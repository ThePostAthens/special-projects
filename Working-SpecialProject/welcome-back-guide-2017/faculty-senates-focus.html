Faculty Senate's Focus

08.24.17

Faculty Senate is preparing for the Escobedo hearing and focusing on budget and facilities

Sarah Penix / For The Post


Since the Ohio University Faculty Senate meeting in May, Duane Nellis became OU’s 21st president, the Board of Trustees met and the faculty hearing for English professor Andrew Escobedo was scheduled.

 

Before the new Faculty Senate year begins, the faculty hearing for Escobedo, who is facing dismissal for sexual harassment, will take place publicly on Sept. 1-2. An Office of Equity and Civil Rights Compliance investigation into Escobedo’s actions found that he had engaged in a pattern of sexual misconduct for more than a decade. That involved buying female students drinks at bars and then touching them sexually without consent, according to a previous Post report.

Ben Bates, the chair of the Promotion and Tenure Committee, and Jacqueline Wolf, Executive Committee at-large senator, have been helping Robin Muhammad, the chair of the hearing committee, with logistics, rules, faculty handbook language and contacting hearing participants. Witnesses for the hearing have been contacted, but arrangements are still being made about who will be in attendance and who will use Skype.

“Until we have heard from all the witnesses, it’s just now this summer that it’s starting to gel, so certainly by mid August we will know exactly how we’re going to proceed and who’s going to be there,” Wolf said.

OU has not held a faculty hearing in nearly 30 years.

After the hearing, Faculty Senate will hold the first meeting of the semester on Sept. 11 at 7 p.m. in Walter Hall 235 and will introduce new administrative members. President Nellis and interim Executive Vice President and Provost David Descutner will continue the senate tradition of beginning the meeting by fielding questions.

Budget and facilities will be important topics for the university Fall Semester, as the Budget Planning Council, which examines major financial drivers of the university and their costs, works to determine the budget for next year, Faculty Senate Vice Chair David Thomas said.

 

“There’s what we call operational money, and there’s capital money,” Thomas said. “The operational money is going to possibly pay my raise, or not, or buy us a new widget. Then there’s capital, which comes every other year. We get a diminishing amount of that because the state is giving out less and less and less money every year.”

 

The plan for the Sook Academic Center, an academic center exclusively for student-athletes, remains a big concern to faculty and administrators because it raises the question of how the university prioritizes money, Thomas said. Although the Board of Trustees previously approved the total project budget of $6.165 million, the amount was increased by $325,000 in June, for a new total budget of $6.49 million, according to a previous Post report.

 

“Right now my sense is that there’s a great deal of skepticism among faculty (about whether) we are in a place where we can do new major construction, and that we really need to be continuing to focus our efforts on deferred maintenance and renovating buildings we have, rather than growing our facilities’ footprint,” Faculty Senate Chair Joe McLaughlin said.

 

Despite budget challenges, the senate said it is “rejuvenated” by new leadership.

 

“I think a lot of the work that’s going to go on this year is transitioning to the new administration (and) making sure we keep open, very good lines of communication with President Nellis,” McLaughlin said. “David Descutner has been around here for a very long time, so I think that will be a really good transition.”
